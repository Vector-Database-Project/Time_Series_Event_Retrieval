Literature Review and Research Gap

Similar-event retrieval is common in sensor streams (maintenance vibration, IMU, ECG), often under metadata filters, so outcomes depend on embeddings plus indexing and filtered execution behavior.

Time-series embeddings and representation learning.

Time-series representation learning has shifted toward reusable embeddings. TS2Vec uses hierarchical contrastive learning and performs strongly across benchmarks, supporting general-purpose encoders for similarity search [1]. TNC adds an unsupervised objective based on local neighborhood consistency under stationarity assumptions, relevant under drift [2]. CPC motivates predictive, self-supervised objectives for sequence embeddings across modalities [3]. These results support self-supervised embeddings, but do not resolve which representation views transfer reliably across domains.

Supervised models and non-learning baselines.

Strong baselines are needed to separate representation gains from modeling and feature engineering. InceptionTime provides a scalable supervised reference, and penultimate-layer vectors can serve as supervised embeddings when labels exist [4]. tsfresh offers engineered descriptors that remain competitive and help anchor comparisons [5]. DTW remains a canonical alignment similarity reference despite cost at scale [6].

Vector search, indexing, and filtered retrieval behavior.

Large-scale retrieval typically relies on ANN, where index design and constraints can dominate results. HNSW provides strong practical recall-latency tradeoffs in memory [7], while DiskANN extends ANN to large regimes with SSD-aware designs [8]. Faiss offers a broad indexing taxonomy for controlled comparisons [9], and Milvus highlights system concerns such as updates, distributed execution, and hybrid queries [10]. Filtered ANN analysis shows metadata selectivity can substantially change retrieval behavior and conclusions [11].

Research gap.

A gap remains between algorithm-centric results and deployable cross-domain methodology. Many studies optimize one representation on one dataset, or benchmark embeddings without jointly controlling representation view, embedding strategy, and index behavior under filtered workloads. Few works systematically evaluate time-domain, frequency-domain, and fused time-frequency views across supervised, self-supervised, and non-learning approaches, while holding a common retrieval protocol and ANN setup across multiple domains. We address this with a compact, controlled evaluation framework that yields guidance on encoding and retrieval policies that transfer reliably.
