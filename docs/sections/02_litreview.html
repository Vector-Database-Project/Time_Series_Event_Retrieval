Literature Review and Research Gap

Fast retrieval of historically similar events is a recurring requirement in sensor-driven systems, ranging from predictive maintenance (vibration and acoustics) to human sensing (IMU) and biomedical monitoring (ECG). In these settings, analysts often query large archives under metadata constraints, so retrieval quality depends not only on representation quality but also on the interaction between embeddings, indexing, and filtering behavior.

Time-series embeddings and representation learning.

Recent work in time-series representation learning has shifted from task-specific supervised models toward reusable embeddings that transfer across datasets and tasks. TS2Vec introduced hierarchical contrastive learning and demonstrated strong performance across diverse benchmarks, supporting the idea that a single encoder can yield broadly useful representations for heterogeneous time-series analysis and similarity search [1].

Temporal Neighborhood Coding (TNC) provides a related unsupervised objective based on neighborhood consistency under local stationarity assumptions, which is relevant for real sensor streams where distributions drift and operating regimes change [2].

Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC) remains a foundational framework motivating forecasting-oriented latent objectives across modalities, and it informs the broader design space of self-supervised objectives for sequence embeddings [3].

These works motivate self-supervised embeddings as strong candidates for retrieval, but they do not by themselves establish which representation choices generalize reliably across domains.

Supervised models and non-learning baselines.

Robust supervised and classical baselines remain essential for retrieval studies because they anchor conclusions about representation quality and transfer. InceptionTime shows that modern convolutional architectures can achieve high accuracy and scalability in supervised time-series classification; penultimate-layer vectors can serve as a practical supervised embedding baseline when labels are available [4].

On the non-learning side, tsfresh provides a comprehensive library of engineered time-series descriptors that are often competitive in applied settings and help separate gains from representation learning versus gains from strong feature engineering [5].

Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) remains a canonical similarity measure for local alignment and is still a relevant reference point for pattern-matching regimes, despite its computational cost at scale [6].

Together, these baselines provide a disciplined way to evaluate whether learned embeddings offer consistent advantages across domains and representation views.

Vector search, indexing, and filtered retrieval behavior.

Large-scale retrieval typically relies on approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search, where observed performance can be dominated by index design and query constraints rather than embedding quality alone. HNSW introduced a graph-based ANN approach with strong practical recall-latency trade-offs in memory-resident settings [7].

DiskANN extended ANN search to large-scale regimes by leveraging SSD-aware designs to reduce memory pressure while preserving competitive latency and recall [8].

Faiss provides a broad indexing taxonomy and implementation guidance that is useful for controlled index comparisons in experimental pipelines [9]. At the system layer, Milvus highlights operational considerations such as updates, distributed execution, and hybrid query behavior that influence real deployment outcomes beyond raw nearest-neighbor metrics [10].

Importantly, recent filtered ANN analysis shows that metadata constraints can substantially change retrieval behavior; selectivity and execution planning effects can dominate conclusions drawn from unfiltered benchmarks [11].

Research gap.

Across these literatures, a gap remains between algorithm-centric results and deployable cross-domain retrieval methodology. Many studies optimize a single representation on a single dataset, or benchmark embeddings without jointly controlling representation view, embedding strategy, and retrieval/index behavior under realistic filtered workloads. Fewer works systematically evaluate how time-domain, frequency-domain, and fused time-frequency representations interact with different embedding paradigms and ANN indexing choices across multiple domains under one reproducible protocol. This motivates a methodology-centered study: not a new foundation model, but a compact and controlled evaluation framework that yields actionable guidance on which encoding and retrieval policies remain reliable when transferred across domains.
